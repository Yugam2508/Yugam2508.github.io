§1  ·  Statement

A short note on method. — what I do, and why the boring parts are the good parts.

Filed under:
— Computational physics
— Uncertainty quantification
— Digital twins
— Control systems
— Things that refuse to stay idealised

Most of what I do sits between a simulation and the thing it’s supposed to represent. Physical systems are messy: sensors drift, pumps stutter, gas disperses in ways a mesh can only approximate. Simulations are beautifully idealised, and the interesting problems live in the gap between them.

So I build bridges across that gap — Gaussian-process surrogates that stand in for expensive CFD runs, Polynomial Chaos expansions that turn high-dimensional uncertainty into something tractable, and state-machine algorithms that read noisy drilling data and decide, in real time, whether something has gone wrong.

I studied Engineering Science and Physics because I wanted both halves of the argument: the continuum equations (∇·u = 0, ρDu/Dt = −∇p + μ∇²u) and the experiments that, eventually, fail to satisfy them. Lately I’ve been spending time in Qiskit, in control loops, in order books, and in the slow, careful reading of legacy FORTRAN. They are all, it turns out, the same kind of puzzle.

“Physical systems are messy, simulations are idealised,  —  and the interesting problems live in between.”
